# Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)

Origin: Clayton Christensen (Harvard Business School). People don't buy products — they hire them to do a job. Applied to personal reflection: you don't just "have goals" — each goal is hired to do a job in your life.

## When to Use

- Questioning why you're pursuing a particular goal
- Goal feels hollow or obligation-driven
- Multiple goals competing for attention — which matters most?
- Evaluating whether a new opportunity fits your life

## The Process

### Step 1: Identify the "job" behind the goal
Every goal serves an underlying need. Ask: "What job am I hiring this goal to do?"

| Stated Goal | Possible Job |
|-------------|-------------|
| "Lose 10kg" | Feel confident, have energy for kids, reduce health anxiety |
| "Get promoted to L6" | Financial security, respect from peers, prove capability |
| "Read 20 books" | Intellectual stimulation, escape, signal identity |
| "Save for early retirement" | Security against uncertainty, freedom to take risks later |

### Step 2: Evaluate job fit
Is this goal actually the best way to get the job done?
- Are there other "candidates" that could do the job better?
- Is the goal over-specified? (Maybe any form of exercise does the job, not just "lose 10kg")
- Is the goal under-specified? (Maybe "get promoted" won't actually deliver the respect you want)

### Step 3: Check for competing jobs
Goals often have multiple jobs. Sometimes they conflict:
- "Get promoted" jobs: financial security + intellectual challenge + status
- But the promotion path might kill the intellectual challenge job

### Step 4: Prioritize by job importance
Which underlying jobs are most important to you right now? Prioritize goals that serve those jobs.

## Forces of Progress

Four forces act on every goal decision:

```
PUSH toward new goal ←→ PULL of current situation
   (dissatisfaction)         (habit, comfort)

PULL toward new goal ←→ PUSH away from new goal
   (attraction)              (anxiety, risk)
```

All four forces are always present. When you're stuck, identify which force is dominant.

## Example Application

**Situation:** "Should I pursue the ML systems learning roadmap or double down on distributed systems expertise?"

**JTBD Analysis:**
- ML systems job: stay relevant in shifting industry, expand career options
- Distributed systems job: deepen moat, become the expert others consult
- Real question: Which job is more important — relevance or depth?

**Forces:**
- Push from current: "Pure distributed systems feels like it's becoming commodity"
- Pull toward ML: "ML infra is where the growth is"
- Pull of current: "I'm already deep in distributed systems, switching has cost"
- Push from ML: "Everyone's doing ML — will I just be mediocre at it?"

## Pitfalls

- **Confusing the goal with the job**: The goal is the vehicle, not the destination.
- **Too many jobs per goal**: If a goal is serving 5 jobs, it's probably not doing any of them well.
- **Ignoring emotional jobs**: "Look smart" and "feel secure" are real jobs. Don't pretend they don't exist.

## Cross-validates with

- **Ikigai** — JTBD asks "what job does this goal do?" Ikigai asks "does this goal serve all four dimensions?"
- **First Principles** — JTBD reveals the real need; First Principles finds the best solution for it
